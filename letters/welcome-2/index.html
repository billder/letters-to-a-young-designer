Letters to a young designer

Beginning your career in design or interested in learning what it’s all about? Welcome aboard to a flexible, impactful, evolving, rewarding, and often confusing way to make your way through life.

If I leave you with just three things, it’s this:

  • Your career, peaking in the 2050s, will have opportunities difficult for us to imagine today.
  • The world of design is much bigger than pixels that fit in rectangles.
  • Design can make people’s lives better. It can also make them much worse. Considering the state of the world, let's focus on better, or at least not worse.

Let's get started.

This collection of essays is for those of you starting your design career, written from the perspective of someone in the last third of his. This project expanded from a single question: What future career possibilities may exist for today’s young designers?

The questions we will ask herein are

  • What will the future hold for today's young designers?
  • How may the field change?
  • What new career opportunities may arise?

But naturally, these questions create bigger questions.

  • What does it mean to design?
  • How will an increasingly challenging world affect things?
  • How will new technologies contribute?
  • How will our sense of reality change?

Core points.

Time

  • Today’s young designers will be at the peak of their careers in the 2050s.
  • You can make the future real by anchoring your personal timeline with the simplest math.
  • Generations

Design

  • “Design” is a complex and evolving word.
  • We are born into different baseline realities.

Technology

  • Technology changes how we sense reality. Examples from the past and future.

Context

  • The megaproblems of the world will come to a head in the coming decades.

Now what

This will be perpetually evolving and unfinished. I'm aiming for depth over polish.

This collection will be hypertext because

  • I struggled to shape it linearly into a book and this helps me publish faster.
  • It's a callback to the early web and even earlier to Ted Nelson's Xanadu.

The core principle here is that most discussions around "Design" today pigeonhole it as the process of producing screens to be developed, forcing it to be defined as a relationship between engineering and product management.

What's being lost is design as a means to address a problem.
